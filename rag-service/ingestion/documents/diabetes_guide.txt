Diabetes is a chronic condition where the body cannot properly regulate blood sugar levels.

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the pancreas produces little or no insulin.

Type 2 diabetes occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough insulin.

Normal fasting blood glucose is below 100 mg/dL. Prediabetes is 100-125 mg/dL. Diabetes is diagnosed at 126 mg/dL or higher.

Symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination, excessive thirst, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow-healing sores.

BMI above 25 is considered overweight and above 30 is obese. Obesity significantly increases the risk of Type 2 diabetes.

The diabetes pedigree function measures the genetic influence of diabetes in family history. Higher values indicate stronger family history.

Regular physical activity improves insulin sensitivity and helps control blood sugar levels.

A diabetes-friendly diet limits refined carbohydrates, sugary drinks, and processed foods while emphasizing vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that allows cells to use glucose for energy.

High insulin resistance means the body's cells do not respond properly to insulin, leading to elevated blood sugar.

Blood pressure control is important in diabetes management as hypertension increases risk of diabetic complications.

Skin thickness measurements can indicate insulin resistance as excess fat deposits affect subcutaneous tissue.

Pregnancy can trigger gestational diabetes due to hormonal changes that affect insulin function.

Regular monitoring of blood glucose, HbA1c, blood pressure, and cholesterol is essential for diabetes management.
